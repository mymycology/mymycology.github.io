Terms

Fungus

An organism belonging to the kingdom Fungi, separate from plants and animals. It is eukaryotic, doesn’t photosynthesize, and obtains nutrients by absorbing them from its surroundings. It may live as a saprotroph, symbiont, or parasite. Fungi can include yeasts, molds, rusts, mildews, mushrooms, and more. They can be macroscopic or microscopic, unicellular or multicellular, and reproduce sexually or asexually. There are possibly more than 3 million species worldwide, but only 148,000 have been described.

Fruiting body

The reproductive part of a fungus (such as a mushroom) that produces, contains, and releases spores.

Mycelium

The root-like structure of vegetative tissue that makes up the feeding and growing part of a fungus, consisting of a network of fine filaments called hyphae. Mycelium allows a fungus to absorb nutrients from its environment, and it may produce fruiting bodies such as mushrooms. It plays important roles in soil health, agriculture, plant growth, and the carbon cycle.

Hypha

A branching, thread-like filament of a fungus; hyphae collectively make up a fungus’s mycelium.

Mycorrhiza

A mutualistic, close physical association between a fungus and the roots of a plant that is beneficial for both organisms. The fungus allows the plant roots to take up nutrients more efficiently, while the plant provides energy for the fungus. This form of mutualism is highly common, as it is estimated to occur in more than 90% of terrestrial plants.

Mycology

The scientific study of fungi. For centuries, mycology was considered a branch of botany, but it eventually diverged as its own field with compounding research on the taxonomy of fungi. Key figures within the history of mycology include Franciscus van Sterbeeck, Pier Antonio Micheli, Louis Pasteur, Robert Koch, Elias Fries, Christian Hendrik Persoon, Anton de Bary, and Albert Bernhard Frank.

Ethnomycology

The study of traditional knowledge, customs, and cultural attitudes of a people concerning fungi.

Mycophobia and mycophilia

Two (seemingly) dichotomous terms, coined by Gordon and Valentina Wasson, to describe cultural attitudes towards fungi; mycophobia is a negative, fungus-fearing attitude, while mycophilia is a positive, fungus-loving attitude.
